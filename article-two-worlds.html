Two Worlds, One Word: “Fast”

What “move fast” means in a boardroom — and what it means at 2 a.m. next to a machine that won’t run.

By Randy Alsup•July 1, 2026

There’s a version of AI that lives in the boardroom, and a version that lives on the plant floor. Both are real. Both matter. But they are not the same world, and the advice that works in one can be quietly dangerous in the other.

In the boardroom, the promise is speed. Solve it in days, not months. Break the silos. Cut the meetings. Turn AI hype into action before your competitor does. And for that world — strategy, alignment, decision-making, culture — the promise is often exactly right. Teams do get stuck in endless discussion. Alignment is the bottleneck. Moving fast, together, is a genuine competitive edge when the thing you’re moving is a decision.

Then there’s the other world.

On the floor, the clock runs differently

A line goes down at 2 a.m. A drive throws a fault code nobody at the site has seen before. The signals conflict — one reading says overtemp, another says the sensor itself is failing. There is no offsite, no sprint, no room full of people to align. There is one technician, a machine that won’t run, and a batch or a shift or a shipment on the line behind it.

“Move fast” here does not mean decide quickly. It means reason correctly, quickly. Those are not the same thing, and confusing them is how you scrap a batch, trip a safety system, or send someone to open a panel that should have stayed closed.

Speed without grounding isn’t speed. It’s just risk arriving sooner.

This is the part the boardroom version of AI keeps getting wrong. The instinct is to bolt a confident, fast-talking AI onto the problem and let it “accelerate” the fix. But a general AI, asked about a fault it can’t actually verify, doesn’t slow down and admit it doesn’t know. It does the opposite. It answers — fluently, confidently, and sometimes wrong. On familiar-looking equipment it often sounds most sure exactly where it’s least grounded.

In a strategy workshop, a confidently wrong idea gets debated and discarded. No harm done. On the floor, a confidently wrong diagnosis gets acted on — and the cost isn’t a wasted hour, it’s a wasted batch, a damaged asset, or a hurt person.

The plant floor doesn’t reward the fastest answer. It rewards the answer you can trust.

That’s the real distinction between the two worlds. The boardroom optimizes for velocity. The floor optimizes for consequence. And any AI that’s going to earn its place next to a running machine has to be built for consequence first — grounded in the real asset, honest about the limits of what it knows, and bounded so the human who is accountable for that machine stays in authority.

That’s the principle behind everything we build: present, don’t decide. Not because AI can’t be helpful in a hard moment — it can, enormously — but because the moment a fault becomes a decision, that decision has to stay with the person who owns the consequences.

None of this is a knock on moving fast

Speed is good. Alignment is good. Cutting through hype is good. The boardroom consultants selling “days not months” are often right about the world they’re describing.

But the floor is a different world. Down there, the goal was never to answer fast. It was to be right when it counts — and to be honest enough to say “I don’t know yet, let’s look at the machine” instead of guessing with confidence.

The best thing AI can do on a plant floor isn’t to move faster than the technician.

It’s to help the technician reason better — and then get out of the way of their judgment.
